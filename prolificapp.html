The STAR Framework

What you'll be grading

Strong behavioral interview answers follow the STAR framework — Situation, Task, Action, Result. As a grader, your job is to identify which parts of the candidate's response correspond to each component, and how much time they spend on each.
SSituation~20%
The context or background — where and when the story took place. Look for setting, timeframe, and relevant background detail.
e.g. "During my internship last summer, our team was working on a consulting project…"
TTask~10%
The specific goal or responsibility the candidate held. Look for what they were personally accountable for.
e.g. "I was responsible for coordinating the final deliverable across three offices."
AAction~60%
The specific steps the candidate took — their choices, behaviors, and decisions. This should be the bulk of the answer.
e.g. "I set up daily check-ins, created a shared timeline, and proactively flagged blockers."
RResult~10%
The outcome — what happened as a direct result of the candidate's actions. Look for measurable or concrete outcomes.
e.g. "We submitted on time and the client extended the engagement."
The percentages above reflect ideal time allocation. Action (~60%) should dominate — it's the most telling part of any behavioral answer.
Below is an example of a strong STAR response to the question: "Tell me about a time you had to meet a challenging deadline." Notice how each part maps to a STAR component.
[S] During an internship I did this past summer, we had a project where [T] the deadline was in about a couple of weeks and we had to coordinate between teams — [S] not just the Chicago office, but also Dallas and New York. [T] The project was essentially putting together a consulting presentation to help a nonprofit improve their revenue for the upcoming year. And [A] what I did to meet the deadline was make sure to touch base with the team every day, whether over a meeting or through our Teams chat, and [A] made sure that every single person knew what they were going to work on that day. [A] I would also check in throughout each day to make sure we were on schedule and figure out where people needed help or could assist others. And yeah, [R] ultimately that led to us being able to meet our deadline, and with some extra time, [R] we were able to go above and beyond and seek feedback before even presenting to the project manager.
Notice that Action makes up the majority of the response — multiple sentences describing specific steps the candidate personally took.
Tips for Grading STAR Responses
🎯
Focus on what the candidate personally did
Action should dominate. If a candidate mostly describes the situation or outcome with little detail on their own steps, that's a weak response.
🔍
Look for specificity, not just structure
Good answers name specific tools, decisions, or outcomes — not vague claims like "I helped the team" or "things went well."
⚖️
A phrase can span multiple components
Sometimes a sentence contains both Situation and Task, or blends Action and Result. Highlight the most dominant component the text is communicating.
📊
Use the time balance as a quality signal
Responses that are mostly Situation/Task with little Action typically score lower. Responses with rich, specific Action content score higher.
✅
Not every response will have all four components
Some candidates skip or combine components. Only highlight what is clearly present — don't force a label onto ambiguous text.

The Four Frames
S Situation
The context, background, or setting of the story.
Look for: time, place, role, or general environment — "During an internship I did this past summer…", "my sophomore fall, I took on a few different responsibilities"
T Task
The specific problem, goal, or deadline the student had to meet.
Look for: what was required or the conflict faced — "we had a project where the deadline was in about a couple weeks", "the goal here was essentially for me to remain on top of all my commitments"
A Action
The specific, observable steps the student personally took to solve the problem.
Look for: first-person "I" statements describing active steps — "make sure to touch base with the team every day", "I would also try to check in throughout each day"
R Result
The outcome, impact, or learning that occurred because of the action.
Look for: finishing, grades, feedback, or reflections — "ultimately that led to us being able to meet our deadline", "what I took away from that is that planning goes such a long way"

Q1 — Time Management & Working Under Pressure

"I broke the project into daily milestones. I proactively asked my manager to reassign two non-essential tasks, and I used a shared calendar to keep the team updated. We delivered the project two days early."
Participant explicitly describes strategic planning, resource negotiation, and a successful, quantifiable outcome.
4 Good Between 3 and 5 — some organization, lacks strategic negotiation
Response falls between 3 and 5. They show some organization but lack high-level strategic negotiation or proactive resource management.
3 Average — Read this first Brute force effort, met deadline but no systemic planning
"I just stayed late every night and worked through the weekend until it was done. I drank a lot of coffee and pushed through it. I met the deadline, but I was exhausted."
Participant relies on brute-force effort and longer hours rather than systemic planning or communication.
2 Marginal Between 1 and 3 — struggled significantly, barely finished
Response falls between 1 and 3. They struggled significantly but barely managed to finish.
1 Poor Poor planning, panic, or missed the deadline
"I panicked because there wasn't enough time. I tried to do it all at the last minute, ignored my other work, and ended up missing the deadline."
Participant describes poor planning, panic, or failing to meet the objective entirely.
Q2 — Prioritization & Organization
"Tell me about a time when you had too many things to do and you were required to prioritize your tasks."
5 Excellent Logical framework + stakeholder communication + delegation
"I reviewed all my tasks and categorized them by urgency and importance. I communicated with stakeholders to push back the deadline on lower-priority items, and I delegated one task to a teammate. All critical tasks were completed on time."
Participant describes applying a logical framework, communicating with stakeholders, and successfully managing expectations.
4 Good Between 3 and 5 — prioritized well, no stakeholder management
Response falls between 3 and 5. They prioritized well but did not actively manage external stakeholder expectations.
3 Average — Read this first Basic to-do list, arbitrary prioritization
"I wrote down a to-do list and just started working on whatever was due first. I worked really fast and managed to get most of the things done by the end of the day."
Participant uses basic, arbitrary prioritization methods without evaluating strategic importance.
2 Marginal Between 1 and 3 — attempted to organize, dropped tasks
Response falls between 1 and 3. They attempted to organize but still dropped some important tasks.
1 Poor Chaotic multitasking, overwhelmed, dropped responsibilities
"I tried to multitask and do a little bit of everything at once. I got completely overwhelmed, shut down, and forgot to submit the most important assignment."

You will evaluate each response using two separate tools: the STAR Structure Checklist (measures how the answer was formatted) and the BARS (measures the quality of the competency demonstrated). Grade them independently — a well-structured STAR response can still score a 1 on BARS.
5 Key Rules
1
Look for evidence, not eloquence. Do not penalize for stuttering or filler words, and do not reward smooth delivery. Grade strictly on the presence or absence of the required elements.
2
Track accumulation (STAR scores). Each level (1–5) builds on the previous one. A participant earns a higher score only by successfully accumulating the required elements defined in the rubric.
3
Set your BARS baseline. Always read the Score 3 (Average) anchor first before grading. Then decide if the response is above, below, or at that level. Give a 1, 3, or 5 for a clear match; give a 2 or 4 if the response falls strictly between two anchors.
4
Avoid Halo bias. Grade BARS quality completely independent of structure. A beautifully formatted STAR answer can still demonstrate poor strategy. A rambling response can still show excellent judgment.
5
STAR Checklist is presence-only. Mark Yes if the component appears at all — even weakly. "I just worked faster" still earns Yes for Action. Reserve the 1–5 detail score for quality.
Your Workflow
1 Read the full transcript once without annotating.
2 Go back and highlight + label all STAR spans (S, T, A, R).
3 In the grading panel, mark Yes/No for each element and assign a detail score (1–5).
4 Read the BARS Score 3 anchor, then assign a BARS score (1–5) for overall quality.
5 Hit Save, then switch to Q2 and repeat the process.
